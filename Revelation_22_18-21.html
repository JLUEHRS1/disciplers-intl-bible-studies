REVELATION 22:18-21

Eternity: Final Testimony & Benediction

Observation:

[John] says that God will add the plagues in this book to anyone who adds to its words, and will take away the tree of life from anyone who takes away from its words; He who testifies these things says, “I am coming soon.”  [John] closes by extending the grace of the Lord Jesus to all.

 

Previous Observation:

The Alpha and Omega will come soon and give to all according to what they did; those washing their robes are blessed, for they have the right to the tree of life.

Interpretation:

In this verse, John gives a final warning to his readers.  In his day, the only way written material could get to an intended audience was for multiple copies to be handwritten, and then distributed.  Some scholars say that John is concerned about reproductions of this book, and wants to be sure that copyists do not edit what he has written.  Those holding this view believe John is concerned with the integrity of the text, because the words he has recorded were inspired by the Holy Spirit.  Therefore, he must ensure that copyists reproduce it accurately.  Others draw meaning from this verse in the light of 22:7, which says, “Blessed is he who heeds to words of the prophecy of this book” (see 22:7).  So, one may say that a blessing comes to those who keep the prophecies John has recorded, and a curse comes to those who alter God’s word by adding to it in any way.  The plagues recorded in this book are horrific, so to say that God will add those plagues to those who add to His word underscores the importance of preserving and presenting God’s word correctly.

Questions:

What is the most serious warning you ever received?  Who issued it, what were the consequences of disregarding it, and how did you respond to it?  Do you know of any churches that have added certain teachings to their doctrine that are not found in Scripture?  If so, give examples.

John goes a step further here by telling readers what they will forfeit if they take away from the words he has written, which essentially are the words God has spoken.  Just as the curse noted in the last verse is as bad as bad can be, the loss noted in this verse is also as bad as bad can be, for the “tree of life” corresponds to salvation.  If anyone takes away from the words of the prophecy, “God will take away his share in the tree of life and in the holy city” (RSV).  The word xulon (“tree”) is translated as “wood” in 18:12, and is used with reference to the cross in Acts 5:30, 10:39; 13:29; 1 Pet. 2:24.  Again, John warns of the serious consequences that come to anyone who manipulates the words of this book in any way.  A warning or curse to curtail alterations in a book was not uncommon in ancient times.  The author of Deuteronomy, a work that purports to be the revelation of God to Moses, includes two solemn warnings against additions or omissions (see Deut. 4:2; 12:32).  A similar warning occurs in 2 Pet. 3:16, where Peter says that heretics twisted Paul’s letters, as they did the other scriptures, to “their own destruction.”  In Gal. 1:8 Paul issues a strong warning of his own when he writes, “Even if we, or an angel from heaven, should preach to you a gospel contrary to what we have preached to you, he is to be accursed!”  He then repeats the same warning in the next verse for emphasis.  In the ancient times all books were hand-copied by scribes, and everyone knew how easy it was for a scribe to make mistakes; therefore, it was customary to insert a solemn warning against change at the end of a book.  From the beginning, John has expected readers to regard this book as divinely inspired, for in the third verse he calls it “The Revelation of Jesus Christ” (1:3).  Accordingly, blessings are pronounced upon those who consider it as such and obey it (see 1:3; 22:7).   Conversely, as John notes here, those who detract from God’s word will experience a double curse by having no share in the tree of life or in the holy city.  This verse has nothing to do with the eternal security of believers, for the context does not support the view of some who think that Christians can lose their salvation.  Yet, the warnings in vv. 18-19 are not idle threats, but serious consequences for the foolhardy.

22:20

He who testifies to these things says, “Yes, I am coming quickly.”  Amen.  Come, Lord Jesus.

 

The one testifying to these things says, “Yes, I come quickly.”  Amen.  Come, Lord Jesus.

 

testifying 3140 martureo (see v. 18)

quickly 5035 tachu - Quickly, swiftly, speedily, fast.

Amen 281 amen - Amen, truly.  Transliterated from Hebrew - firm, steady or trustworthy.

 

John includes a final testimony from the Lord Jesus, who assures readers by affirming this fact: “Yes, I am coming soon” (NIV).  For the third time in this last chapter, Jesus gives His solemn assurance that He will return soon (see also, vv. 7, 12).  John answers with two short responses.  First, he says, “Amen.”  This word, which is transliterated from directly from Hebrew into Greek and English, appeared frequently at the end of sentences as an adverb to mean certainly, surely or truly.  Immediately after Jesus proclaims His imminent return, John’s responds with a one-word affirmation, “Amen” (i.e. “Let it be so”), followed by this heartfelt appeal: “Come, Lord Jesus.”   Clearly, John is ready for Jesus to return right away.  This is understandable, especially in light of the fact that John is writing Revelation while suffering in exile on the remote island of Patmos (see 1:9).  Regardless of John’s circumstances, his response to Jesus should be the response of all Christians in all situations, in all places and at all times.

Questions:

Are you ready for Jesus to return now?  Why or why not?

 

22:21

The grace of the Lord Jesus be with all.  Amen.

 

The grace of the Lord Jesus be with all.  Amen.

 

grace 5485 charis - Grace, favor, kindness.

Amen 281 amen (see v. 20)

 

John’s closing words are an extension of grace on behalf of Jesus to all believers, followed by a final “Amen.”  The NIV substitutes “all” with “God’s people,” in spite of the fact that panton (“all”) is all that appears in the Greek.  The final “Amen” appears in some, but not all, Greek manuscripts.  There is both pathos and glory in the way in which Revelation ends.  Amidst the terrible persecutions in his lifetime, John longed for the speedy return of Jesus.  Yet, neither he nor believers from every generation since the 1st century have realized that hope.  Nevertheless, Christians remain hopeful, always trusting in this promise of the Lord Jesus Christ: “I am with you always, even to the end of the age” (Matt. 28:20).  Come what may, John was certain about of the grace of the Lord Jesus, and equally certain that it is sufficient for all people of all times.

Questions:

How has God’s grace affected your life?  How can you extend God’s grace to others today?

 

Application:

To join with John by saying, “Come, Lord Jesus.”
